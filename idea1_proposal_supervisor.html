FYDP · Idea 1 — Research Proposal

Trust-Calibrated Multi-Agent Scientific Deliberation
for Mitigating Sycophantic Consensus in LLM Reasoning

Final Year Design Project — Research Proposal

Domain: Agentic AI · Multi-Agent Systems · Trustworthy NLP

1. Background and Motivation

Getting several large language models (LLMs) to debate a question before agreeing on an answer — called Multi-Agent Debate (MAD) — is a popular way to improve factual accuracy (Du et al., 2023). But these debates have a real weakness: an agent often drops a correct answer just to agree with a confident-sounding majority, and the group ends up with a confidently wrong consensus (Yao et al., 2025). Because today's models are trained on overlapping data, they tend to make the same mistakes, so a plain majority vote can quietly bury a correct minority — in fact the minority turns out to be right in roughly one in four disagreements (He et al., 2026). In scientific or clinical question answering, a confidently wrong group answer is more harmful than an honest "I'm not sure."

2. Problem Statement

In current debate systems, how much an agent influences the final answer depends on how many other agents agree with it — not on whether what it says is actually true. There is no mechanism that adjusts each agent's influence during the debate based on whether its claims can be checked against real evidence. As a result, a hallucinating majority can override a correct, well-supported minority. This is the problem we want to solve.

3. Research Gap

Recent work has measured this failure (Yao et al., 2025; He et al., 2026) and built efficiency-focused debate controllers (iMAD, Fan et al., 2025). What is still missing is a mechanism that actually mitigates it — an evidence-grounded trust rule that re-weights agents during the debate — together with a reproducible way to measure whether such a mechanism helps. That gap is what this project targets.

4. Our Idea (the core of the project)

A group of open-weight LLMs from different model families debate a question. As they argue, each agent's claims are broken into small factual statements and checked against scientific sources such as Semantic Scholar, arXiv, and PubMed. Each agent then carries a trust score that goes up when its claims are supported by evidence and down when they are contradicted, kept within bounds so that no agent is silenced and none is allowed to dominate. Instead of a plain majority vote, the final answer is produced by trust-weighted aggregation, which lets a well-supported minority survive against a confident but unsupported majority. A lightweight check decides when the full debate is even needed, so easy questions are answered directly.

5. Research Question and Objectives

Research question: when a debate faces a planted, confident-but-wrong majority, does evidence-grounded trust weighting keep correct minority answers alive better than a plain majority vote and other common methods?

To answer this, the objectives are to:

  1. design a bounded, evidence-weighted trust-update rule driven by supported versus contradicted claims;
  2. build the pipeline around it — claim decomposition, evidence retrieval, scoring, and trust-weighted aggregation;
  3. create a small, reproducible evaluation setup, including a controlled way to inject a false consensus and three metrics (CCR, MPR, ECR); and
  4. compare the method against strong baselines and report the results honestly, including where it does not help.

6. How We Plan to Build and Test It

The system uses three agents from different open-weight model families (the exact models will be chosen later, once we confirm what runs well on the hardware available to us). Everything runs at inference time — we do not train any model — which keeps the project light and realistic for an FYDP. Each claim is split into atomic statements, evidence is retrieved from academic sources, a cross-encoder scores how relevant it is, and claims that cannot be verified are set aside rather than trusted.

For testing, we use BrokenMath, BrokenArXiv, and HLE for the adversarial sycophancy cases and GPQA and MMLU-Pro for ordinary accuracy. The heart of the evaluation is a controlled way of injecting a wrong "expert consensus" into the debate, checked for consistency before the main runs and reported over several seeds. Three metrics tell the story: CCR (how often a correct agent collapses to the wrong answer), MPR (how often a correct minority survives to the output), and ECR (how well an agent's trust matches whether it is actually right). We compare against single-agent baselines, standard debate, self-consistency, Mixture-of-Agents, and a confidence-triggered debate in the style of iMAD.

7. Expected Contributions and Outcomes

  • An evidence-grounded trust-weighting mechanism that reduces sycophantic consensus collapse in debate.
  • A small, open, reusable evaluation setup (the injection protocol plus the CCR/MPR/ECR metrics and baselines).
  • A working prototype and a clear picture of when evidence-grounded trust helps and when it does not, with failure analysis.
  • A solid thesis and, with good results, a paper suitable for a good conference or journal in the area.

8. Key References (what they did)

  • Du et al. (2023) — introduced multi-agent debate and showed it improves factual and reasoning accuracy. This is our base method.
  • Yao et al. (2025) — showed and measured that agents become sycophantic in debate and collapse to a wrong majority. This defines the problem we mitigate.
  • He et al. (2026) — found that a correlated majority suppresses the correct minority in about one in four disagreements. This motivates protecting the minority.
  • Fan et al. (2025), iMAD — learns when to trigger a debate for efficiency. A baseline; it decides whether to debate, not who to trust.
  • Wang et al. (2024), Mixture-of-Agents — combines several models with equal-weight aggregation. A baseline; it has no evidence-based trust.
