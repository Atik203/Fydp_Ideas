Evaluation Framework

How Each Idea Was Assessed

All five proposals were evaluated using a consistent six-dimension rubric aligned with Washington Accord engineering standards and Q1 publication criteria.

🔬
Novelty

Is the research gap real, explicit, and not vague? Does the solution introduce a new mechanism, metric, dataset, or architectural component? "We apply X to Y" is not novel — we require a new formal contribution that cannot be derived from existing work.

✅
Feasibility

Can a single undergraduate team execute this in 12 months without heroic assumptions about compute, data access, or domain expertise? Scored against realistic resource constraints: single GPU, public datasets, open-source frameworks.

📄
Q1 Publication Potential

Would a strong execution of this idea produce a paper competitive at ACL, EMNLP, NeurIPS, ICML, or JMLR? Assessed against current reviewer expectations: novel contribution + rigorous evaluation + reproducible experiments + statistically significant results.

🎓
PhD Impact

Does this project create a strong, memorable research identity for PhD applications? Does it align with open faculty positions at MIT CSAIL, Stanford HAI, CMU LTI, Oxford, ETH Zurich? Is the research direction fundable and expanding through 2030?

⚠️
Risk Control (Inverted)

Score 5 = Very Low Risk (desirable). What is the probability of producing nothing publishable? Key risks: data access bottlenecks, expert recruitment failures, scope creep, compute cost overruns, and upstream dependency on proprietary APIs.

⚙️
Engineering Complexity (Inverted)

Score 5 = Very Low Complexity (desirable). Assessed at Washington Accord graduate level: system integration difficulty, number of novel components that must be built from scratch, and the gap between available open-source tools and what the project requires.
