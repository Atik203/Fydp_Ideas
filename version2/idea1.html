Core Idea

What This Project Proposes

We propose TCMAD — a Trust-Calibrated Multi-Agent Debate framework where specialized LLM agents argue for and against scientific claims, dynamically assign credibility weights to each other's arguments based on evidence quality and logical consistency, and converge on a calibrated verdict with explicit epistemic uncertainty quantification. Unlike existing debate systems (Du et al., 2023) where agents are symmetric and uncalibrated, TCMAD introduces a Trust Score Module (TSM) that makes agent authority a first-class variable in the reasoning process. The system produces not just a verdict but a confidence-calibrated resolution grounded in a novel SciContra benchmark of annotated contradictory scientific claim pairs.

🔴
Problem Statement
Real-world urgency + engineering complexity
  • Scientific misinformation is accelerating across medical, climate, and policy domains — with direct policy consequences
  • LLMs hallucinate plausible-sounding but factually incorrect scientific verdicts at rates of 18–32% on domain-specific benchmarks
  • No AI system can reason across multiple conflicting studies simultaneously — single-pass LLM inference collapses conflicting evidence into a single unsupported assertion
  • Clinicians and policymakers cannot trust AI-generated scientific summaries without calibrated uncertainty — the "I am 73% confident" output does not exist in any current system
  • Peer review is overwhelmed — 2.5M papers published annually, review turnaround averages 6–18 months, automated contradiction detection at scale is an unsolved problem
  • Engineering complexity: Requires multi-agent orchestration, dynamic trust scoring, iterative debate convergence, calibration evaluation, and expert human validation — each individually challenging, none previously integrated
📚
Existing Solutions & Their Limitations
Specific papers — not vague references
  • Multi-Agent Debate (Du et al., NeurIPS 2023): Agents debate but all agents are symmetric — no trust scoring, no credibility weighting, no calibration mechanism. Convergence is majority voting, not evidence-grounded.
  • SciFact (Wadden et al., 2020): Single-sentence claim verification against single abstract. No multi-document reasoning, no contradiction resolution, no argumentative structure.
  • MultiVerS (Wadden et al., NAACL 2022): Multi-document but flat text — treats all retrieved documents as equally credible. No epistemic uncertainty output.
  • RAG-based systems (Lewis et al., 2020): Retrieve fresh text but cannot detect or resolve conflicts between retrieved documents. Conflicting evidence is silently averaged or dropped.
  • ChatPDF / Consensus.app: Commercial surface-level tools — no contradiction detection, no calibrated confidence, no structured debate. Not research-grade solutions.
  • GPT-4 Scientific QA: Single-pass inference. No iterative reasoning, no multi-agent structure, 23% hallucination rate on FactScore. Cannot distinguish "strongly supported" from "weakly contested."
🔬
Research Gap — Explicit, Not Vague
What does not exist in the 2024–2025 literature
  • Gap 1: No framework assigns dynamic, evidence-grounded trust scores to debating LLM agents. Trust is implicitly equal in all existing debate systems — a fundamentally flawed assumption when agents cite sources of varying quality.
  • Gap 2: No calibration mechanism exists for multi-agent debate confidence. Existing systems produce binary verdicts without uncertainty quantification — ECE (Expected Calibration Error) of debate systems has never been measured.
  • Gap 3: No benchmark of annotated contradictory scientific claim pairs with ground-truth resolution labels. SciContra does not exist. MultiVerS has contradictions but no structured debate annotations.
  • Gap 4: Agents in existing debate systems are asymmetric only in initial prompt — they do not receive different credibility weights based on argument quality. This makes existing debate a popularity contest, not evidence-weighted reasoning.
  • Gap 5: Epistemic uncertainty output (e.g., "Contested — 67% confidence, 3 supporting vs. 2 contradicting RCTs") is absent from all current scientific reasoning systems. This is the most critical missing output for clinical and policy use.
  • Gap 6: No formal study exists on debate convergence dynamics across N rounds — whether agents converge, oscillate, or diverge based on evidence quality vs. argument fluency.

Proposed Solution

Architecture-Level Design

✨
Novel Components (What Makes This New)
  • Trust Score Module (TSM): Computes a dynamic credibility weight Wi for each agent i at each debate round r. Formally: W_i^r = α·E_i + β·L_i + γ·S_i where E_i = evidence grounding score, L_i = logical consistency score, S_i = source authority score. α, β, γ are learned mixture weights. This is the first principled credibility weighting mechanism in LLM debate.
  • Calibrated Debate Protocol (CDP): Agents receive trust feedback Wir after each round and are instructed to revise arguments to improve their trust score. This is a novel gradient-free update mechanism — agents improve via natural language trust feedback, not weight updates. Convergence criterion: |Wir − Wir−1| < ε.
  • Contradiction Resolution Layer (CRL): A fine-tuned neural module (DeBERTa-v3) that explicitly classifies cross-document evidence pairs as [Supports / Contradicts / Qualifies / Irrelevant]. Trained on SciFact + SciContra. First dedicated contradiction classifier for multi-document scientific text.
  • SciContra Benchmark: 2,400 annotated contradictory scientific claim pairs across biology, medicine, and climate science. Each pair includes: ground-truth resolution label, evidence document set, argumentative annotation (claim/evidence/rebuttal structure). Released publicly — standalone community contribution and guaranteed citation magnet.
⚙️
Why Agentic AI is Architecturally Essential Here
  • Multi-step reasoning is required: Contradictions cannot be resolved in a single forward pass. The system must retrieve → argue → score → revise → re-argue → converge. Each step is conditioned on the previous — this is definitionally agentic behavior.
  • Tool use is mandatory: The Retrieval Agent must call PubMed API and FAISS index. The Trust Scorer must call an external scoring function. These are tool-using agents — not simple prompt chains.
  • Agent specialization is novel: Proponent Agent, Opponent Agent, Trust Scorer Agent, and Resolution Agent have different roles, different prompts, and different output schemas. A single LLM cannot simultaneously argue for, argue against, score, and resolve — specialization is essential.
  • Iterative refinement is non-trivial: The Calibrated Debate Protocol requires agents to receive structured trust feedback and produce revised arguments. This is not achievable with single-pass inference — it requires LangGraph's stateful graph execution.
  • Contrast with Idea #2: Idea #2 (Argumentative Claim Verification) does NOT require Agentic AI — it is a static graph-based NLP system. Here, Agentic AI is the core contribution, not an add-on.

System Architecture & Pipeline
INPUT: Contradictory scientific claim pair
       e.g., ("Ivermectin reduces COVID-19 mortality" vs. "No RCT evidence for Ivermectin benefit")

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STAGE 1: RETRIEVAL AGENT (LangGraph Node 1)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Tool: PubMed API + Semantic Scholar API
  Index: FAISS dense retrieval (Contriever encoder)
  Output: Top-K relevant papers [P_1, P_2, ..., P_K]
          + metadata: {title, year, study_type, sample_size, p_value}

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STAGE 2: DEBATE ROUND r = 1, 2, ..., N
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Agent A (Proponent) → argues FOR the claim
    Input: [Claim_1] + [Retrieved papers] + [Trust feedback from r-1]
    Output: Structured argument {claim, evidence_citations, logical_chain}

  Agent B (Opponent) → argues AGAINST the claim
    Input: [Claim_2] + [Retrieved papers] + [Trust feedback from r-1]
    Output: Structured argument {counter_claim, counter_evidence, rebuttal}

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STAGE 3: TRUST SCORE MODULE (TSM)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  For each agent argument:
    E_i = Evidence Grounding Score
          → citation accuracy + study type quality (RCT=5, Cohort=4, Case=2)
    L_i = Logical Consistency Score
          → DeBERTa NLI score between premises and conclusion
    S_i = Source Authority Score
          → journal impact factor percentile + citation count

    W_i^r = α·E_i + β·L_i + γ·S_i        [α+β+γ=1, learned]

  Convergence check: |W_A^r - W_A^{r-1}| + |W_B^r - W_B^{r-1}| < ε
  If not converged → loop back to Stage 2

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STAGE 4: CONTRADICTION RESOLUTION LAYER (CRL)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Input: Final arguments {A_final, B_final} + Trust weights {W_A, W_B}
  Model: DeBERTa-v3 fine-tuned on SciFact + SciContra
  Resolution: Weighted synthesis of evidence chains
  Calibration: Temperature scaling applied to logits

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
OUTPUT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Verdict:     [Supported | Refuted | Contested]
  Confidence:  0.0 – 1.0  (calibrated, ECE < 0.06 target)
  Trust Weights: W_A = 0.71, W_B = 0.43 (interpretable)
  Evidence Summary: "3 RCTs support, 2 observational studies contradict"
  Debate Trace: Full argument revision history (auditable)

Methodology

Datasets, Models, and Experimental Design

📦
Datasets
  • SciFact (Wadden et al., 2020): 1,409 expert-written claims + 5,183 abstracts. Primary evaluation benchmark. Fully public. Used for baseline reproduction and CRL fine-tuning.
  • MultiVerS (Wadden et al., 2022): Multi-document fact verification across 6 scientific domains. Used for multi-document retrieval baseline.
  • SciContra (custom, this project): 2,400 contradictory claim pairs with expert annotations. Constructed by: (1) automated mining of contradictions in systematic reviews, (2) expert annotation of resolution labels, (3) argument structure annotation using Toulmin schema. 6–8 week construction timeline.
  • FactScore (Min et al., 2023): Used to measure hallucination rate before and after trust calibration. Critical for the hallucination reduction claim.
🤖
Models & Frameworks
  • Llama-3.1-70B: Primary debate agent backbone. Open weights — ensures full reproducibility. No API dependency or rate-limiting risk during experiments.
  • Mistral-Large (7B/8×7B MoE): Secondary backbone for ablation studies — tests whether trust calibration benefits transfer across model families.
  • DeBERTa-v3-large: Trust scoring sub-components (NLI for logical consistency, entailment for evidence grounding). Fine-tuned on SciFact + SciContra CRL component.
  • LangGraph: Stateful multi-agent orchestration. Handles debate loop, convergence checking, and agent state management. Open-source, well-documented, production-grade.
  • FAISS + Contriever: Dense retrieval for PubMed paper search. 26M PubMed abstracts indexed.

📈
Expected Results — Realistic, Not Optimistic
Conservative estimates based on comparable prior work
  • Primary result: TCMAD achieves 83–85% claim accuracy on SciFact, exceeding GPT-4 (71%) and MultiVerS (84.6% F1 — different metric, different task) by a statistically significant margin (p < 0.01)
  • Calibration result: ECE drops from ~0.21 (uncalibrated GPT-4) to <0.06 after temperature scaling + trust calibration — comparable to best-calibrated medical AI systems
  • Convergence result: 76–80% of SciContra debate instances converge within 3 rounds — establishes first empirical characterization of LLM debate dynamics
  • Ablation results: Removing TSM causes 8–12% accuracy drop; removing CRL causes 6–9% drop; N=1 rounds is 15–18% below full system. Each component is validated independently.
  • Human evaluation: Expert agreement κ > 0.78 — comparable to inter-annotator agreement of expert human reviewers on contested scientific claims
  • SciContra release: Benchmark with 2,400 annotated pairs — immediately useful for future work in multi-document scientific NLP

Feasibility Analysis

Honest 1-Year Breakdown

📅
12-Month Timeline
  • Months 1–2 (Foundation): Literature review (Du et al., SciFact, MultiVerS, FactScore). Formal definition of TSM scoring function (Bayesian or information-theoretic grounding — critical). LangGraph environment setup and basic 2-agent debate prototype. Baseline reproduction: GPT-4 on SciFact.
  • Months 3–4 (SciContra Construction): Automated mining of contradiction pairs from systematic reviews (PubMed). Manual expert annotation of 500 pairs (annotation guidelines + inter-annotator agreement). Semi-automated expansion to 2,400 pairs using validated annotation model. Dataset validation and release preparation.
  • Months 5–7 (Core System Development): Trust Score Module implementation + validation. Calibrated Debate Protocol with convergence detection. Contradiction Resolution Layer (DeBERTa fine-tuning on SciFact + SciContra). Temperature scaling calibration. Initial experiments on SciContra held-out set.
  • Months 8–9 (Ablation + Evaluation): Full ablation study (4 conditions × 3 seeds each). Multi-domain evaluation (biology + medicine + climate subsets). Human expert evaluation (recruit 3 domain scientists, structured evaluation protocol). Statistical significance testing.
  • Months 10–12 (Writing + Submission): Paper writing (NeurIPS/ACL format). Internal review by supervisor. Submission. FYDP final report and presentation.
⚠️
Risks & Honest Mitigations
Critical — read before committing
  • Risk 1 (HIGH): TSM formal definition may be vague. A poorly defined trust score will be rejected by NeurIPS reviewers immediately. Mitigation: Spend Weeks 1–4 on formal TSM grounding in Bayesian credibility or information-theoretic evidence weighting. Consult NLP/ML theory literature before implementation.
  • Risk 2 (MEDIUM): SciContra annotation quality. Expert annotation of 2,400 pairs requires domain expertise. Mitigation: Annotate 500 pairs fully manually (inter-annotator κ > 0.75 gate), then use a validated model for semi-automated expansion. Budget 6–8 weeks.
  • Risk 3 (MEDIUM): Compute cost. Llama-3.1-70B requires A100-class GPU. Multi-round debate multiplies inference cost. Mitigation: Use Google Cloud A100 ($2.50/hr) or Lambda Labs A100. Budget $800–1,200 total. Apply for Google TPU Research Credits and AWS Research Credits — both have academic programs.
  • Risk 4 (LOW-MEDIUM): Expert evaluator recruitment. Need 3 domain scientists for human evaluation. Mitigation: Recruit in Month 5 (not Month 9). Offer co-authorship on acknowledgments. Target postdocs at own institution first.
  • Risk 5 (LOW): Debate convergence may be slow or oscillating. Mitigation: Hard cap at N=5 debate rounds. Study convergence dynamics empirically as a research finding — oscillation is itself a publishable result about LLM debate behavior.
