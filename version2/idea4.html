Neuro-Symbolic Rare Disease NLP

Combining neural language understanding with symbolic medical ontology reasoning to identify rare diseases from clinical text — even with near-zero training data — and generate clinician-readable diagnostic explanation chains

Core Idea

What This Project Proposes

We propose NS-RareNLP — a Neuro-Symbolic framework that injects structured medical ontology knowledge (UMLS, SNOMED-CT, ORDO) directly into transformer attention layers, enabling few-shot classification of rare diseases from clinical notes even when fewer than 50 training examples exist. The symbolic component traverses ontology paths from symptom nodes to disease candidate nodes, providing structured diagnostic reasoning that complements neural pattern matching. A novel Ontology-Grounded Encoder bridges the semantic gap between free-text clinical descriptions and formal ontology concepts. The system outputs clinician-readable diagnostic chains: "Patient presents with [symptoms] → UMLS path: [mechanism] → Disease: [candidate] [confidence: 0.84]."

⚠️ Critical Warning: MIMIC-III data access requires PhysioNet credentialing — this process takes 4–8 weeks. Submit the application in Week 1, not Week 8. Failure to do this is the single most common reason medical NLP FYDPs fail to produce results on time. This idea is also best suited for AI+Healthcare or Biomedical Informatics PhD tracks — for a general CS/AI PhD at top programs, Ideas #1 or #2 send a stronger signal.
🔴
Problem Statement
  • 300 million people worldwide are affected by rare diseases — defined as affecting fewer than 1 in 2,000 persons in the EU
  • Average diagnosis takes 4–7 years. 40–50% of rare disease patients receive at least one misdiagnosis. Children are disproportionately affected.
  • Clinical notes describe symptoms in unstructured, ambiguous, abbreviation-heavy natural language — incompatible with structured ontology systems without NLP preprocessing
  • Pure neural models (BERT-based) fail catastrophically with sparse data — rare diseases have <50 clinical notes per disease in any available dataset, often fewer than 10
  • Symbolic ontologies (UMLS, ORDO with 6,000+ rare diseases) contain rich diagnostic knowledge but are completely disconnected from modern NLP pipelines
  • Engineering complexity: Medical ontology integration (UMLS API + Py2neo + SNOMED-CT hierarchy) is non-trivial. Few-shot meta-learning requires Prototypical Networks implementation. Explanation generation requires controlled generation.
📚
Existing Solutions & Limitations
  • BioBERT (Lee et al., 2020) / PubMedBERT (Gu et al., 2021): Strong biomedical NER and classification — but require thousands of labeled examples. Rare disease data is orders of magnitude below this threshold. No symbolic reasoning component.
  • UMLS / SNOMED-CT / ORDO: Richly structured medical knowledge — but static, rule-based, and completely disconnected from neural NLP. Cannot handle the linguistic variability of clinical notes.
  • GPT-4 Medical QA: Impressive general medical knowledge — but hallucination-prone for rare diseases (training data is sparse). Cannot cite ontology paths. Not explainable for clinicians.
  • MetaMap / QuickUMLS: Clinical concept extraction tools — map free text to UMLS concepts. Rule-based, cannot reason across mapped concepts. No disease classification capability.
  • Few-shot medical NLP (Peng et al., 2019): Few-shot learning for medical NLP exists — but without symbolic grounding. Error rate is unacceptably high for rare diseases with no symbolic constraint.
  • Knowledge Graph + NLP hybrids: General KG integration with BERT (Zhang et al., ERNIE 2019) — not designed for medical ontologies, not evaluated on rare disease tasks, no diagnostic reasoning output.
🔬
Research Gap
  • Gap 1: No framework integrates neural NLP with medical ontology (UMLS/ORDO) for rare disease reasoning. The combination is architecturally novel — neither pure neural nor pure symbolic approaches are sufficient alone.
  • Gap 2: Few-shot learning for rare diseases is completely unsupported by symbolic grounding. Existing few-shot medical NLP ignores ontology structure — the most valuable prior knowledge available for rare diseases.
  • Gap 3: Diagnostic explanation generation for rare disease identification does not exist. Clinicians require "show your work" — a confidence score alone is insufficient and dangerous in clinical settings.
  • Gap 4: UMLS and ORDO knowledge is not injected into transformer attention mechanisms. ERNIE-style injection exists for Wikipedia — but medical ontology injection with disease-specific relation types is unstudied.
  • Gap 5: No benchmark specifically for rare disease NLP from clinical notes exists. The RareDis corpus is the closest — but it focuses on entity recognition, not disease classification from symptom descriptions.
  • Gap 6: Neuro-symbolic fusion strategies for medical AI are undefined — should symbolic confidence add to, multiply, or gate neural confidence? This architectural decision has significant downstream consequences and has never been studied.

INPUT: Clinical note (unstructured EHR text)

STAGE 2: ONTOLOGY GROUNDING
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Map UMLS CUIs → ORDO disease candidate paths
  Graph: SNOMED-CT concept hierarchy (Py2neo)

  Traversal:
    Symptom CUI → UMLS semantic relation → Disease candidate
    [Limb Weakness + Denervation + Bulbar symptoms]
    → Motor Neuron Disease path
    → ALS (ORDO:803), Kennedy Disease (ORDO:481), etc.

  Path score: path_score = 1 / (path_length + 1)
              × symptom_coverage_fraction
              × ORDO_specificity_weight

  Top-5 candidate diseases + ontology path evidence

STAGE 3: ONTOLOGY-GROUNDED ENCODER (OGE)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Injection mechanism: Knowledge-aware attention
  (Based on ERNIE framework, adapted for ORDO)

  For each token t_i with UMLS concept c_j:
    KG_embedding = entity_embedding(c_j) concatenated with
                   relation_embedding(r_{ij}) [supports/refutes symptom]
    Modified attention:
    Attn(Q, K, V) → Attn(Q + W_kg·KG_embedding, K, V)

  This explicitly injects "which diseases manifest these symptoms"
  into the transformer's attention computation.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STAGE 4: FEW-SHOT CLASSIFICATION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Method: Prototypical Networks (Snell et al., 2017)
  Setup:  N-way K-shot (N=10 disease candidates, K=5 examples)

  Support set: K examples per disease candidate from MIMIC-III/RareDis
  Query:       Current clinical note

  Prototype: c_k = (1/|S_k|) Σ f_φ(x_i)   [mean embedding per class]
             f_φ = OGE encoder (Stage 3)

  Distance: d(f_φ(x_query), c_k) = Euclidean distance in embedding space
  Probability: p(y=k|x) = softmax(-d(f_φ(x), c_k))

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STAGE 5: NEURAL-SYMBOLIC FUSION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Neural score:   P_neural = Prototypical softmax output
  Symbolic score: P_symbolic = normalized ontology path score

  Fusion strategies (ablation study):
    A: Additive:       P_final = α·P_neural + β·P_symbolic
    B: Multiplicative: P_final = P_neural × P_symbolic (normalized)
    C: Gating:         P_final = g(P_neural)·P_neural + (1-g)·P_symbolic
                       where g = sigmoid(W·[P_neural; P_symbolic; conflict])

  Best strategy selected by validation F1.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

12-Month Timeline
  • Week 1 (CRITICAL): Submit PhysioNet MIMIC-III credentialing application immediately. Also apply for ORDO + UMLS API access. These are gating dependencies.
  • Months 1–2: ORDO + UMLS ontology graph construction (Py2neo). PubMedBERT NER baseline on i2b2. Prototypical Networks prototype (no ontology yet). RareDis corpus preprocessing.
  • Months 3–4: Ontology-Grounded Encoder (knowledge-aware attention injection). Ontology path traversal algorithm. Symptom → UMLS CUI → ORDO disease mapping pipeline.
  • Months 5–6: Few-shot meta-learning with OGE encoder. Neural-symbolic fusion (3 strategies). Core experiments on RareDis + MIMIC-III rare ICD codes.
  • Months 7–8: Explanation generator. Ablation study (NS fusion strategies). Clinician evaluation protocol design. Recruit 3 clinical evaluators (hospital collaboration).
  • Months 9–10: Clinician evaluation (n=40 case studies). Statistical analysis. Full ablation. Domain expert review of explanation quality.
  • Months 11–12: Paper writing (JAMIA / ACL BioNLP format). Submission. FYDP final report.

Risks & Critical Warnings
More risks than Ideas #1-3 — read carefully
  • Risk 1 (HIGH — Gating): MIMIC-III data access. Submit PhysioNet application in Week 1 without exception. Processing takes 4–8 weeks. Missing this step by even 2 weeks can cascade into a 2-month delay. Mitigation: Use i2b2 2010 NER dataset (no credentialing needed) for early experiments. MIMIC-III not needed until Month 4 if i2b2 substitution is planned.
  • Risk 2 (HIGH): Medical ontology engineering is genuinely complex. UMLS has 3.5M concepts, ORDO has 6,000+ disease entries, SNOMED-CT has 350,000+ concepts. Naive integration will produce incorrect mappings. Mitigation: Start with ORDO only (smallest, best-curated). Expand to UMLS in Month 3 only after ORDO pipeline is validated.
  • Risk 3 (MEDIUM): Clinician evaluators are the hardest resource to recruit. Mitigation: Initiate contact with hospital clinical informatics department in Month 2. Offer clinical AI collaboration credit. Identify 5 candidate evaluators and confirm 3 by Month 4.
  • Risk 4 (MEDIUM): Few-shot meta-learning is sensitive to episode design. Wrong N-way K-shot configuration leads to negative results. Mitigation: Use standard Prototypical Networks without modification first. Only tune N and K on validation set. Do not reinvent the meta-learning algorithm.
  • PhD Note (STRATEGIC): This is the best choice for AI+Healthcare or Biomedical Informatics PhD tracks. For general CS/AI PhD at MIT, Stanford, CMU, ETH, Ideas #1 or #2 are significantly stronger signals.
