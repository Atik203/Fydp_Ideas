Core Idea

What This Project Proposes

We propose ArgSciVerify — a framework that constructs Scientific Argument Graphs (SAGs) over retrieved multi-document evidence and reasons over them using Graph Neural Networks to verify scientific claims. Unlike existing systems (SciFact, MultiVerS) that treat verification as flat text classification, ArgSciVerify models the argumentative discourse structure — distinguishing claim nodes, evidence nodes, rebuttal nodes, and qualifier nodes, connected by typed relations (supports/contradicts/qualifies). A novel Epistemic Uncertainty Scorer weights evidence by study design quality (RCT > cohort > observational > opinion), producing calibrated confidence outputs rather than hard labels. A companion SciContra dataset (2,000 annotated contradictory claim pairs) serves as a standalone community contribution.

⚠️ Note on Agentic AI: This idea does NOT require Agentic AI — and that is by design. ArgSciVerify is a static graph-based NLP system. Forcing Agentic AI here would be artificial and architecturally unjustified. The strength of this idea is its clean, reproducible NLP engineering. For Agentic AI, see Idea #1 (Multi-Agent Debate) or Idea #5 (Scientific Verification Agents).
🔴
Problem Statement
Real-world urgency + engineering complexity
  • 1M+ scientific papers published on PubMed annually — systematic manual verification is physically impossible at this scale
  • The replication crisis: 50–70% of published psychology findings fail to replicate (Open Science Collaboration, 2015). Biomedical literature shows similar patterns.
  • Misinformation citing "studies show..." spreads rapidly on social media — existing NLP fact-checkers are trained on news, not science
  • Current scientific NLP (SciFact, MultiVerS) treats verification as text classification — no structure, no reasoning, no uncertainty. Binary labels do not reflect scientific consensus.
  • Meta-analyses require months to years of expert effort — automated support to flag contradictions and structure evidence would transform evidence synthesis
  • Engineering complexity: Scientific Argument Graph construction requires NER, relation extraction, coreference resolution, and graph reasoning — all over technical scientific text with specialized vocabulary
📚
Existing Solutions & Limitations
Specific papers with specific gaps
  • FEVER (Thorne et al., 2018): News fact-checking — completely different domain, style, and reasoning requirements from scientific text. No argument structure, no study design weighting.
  • SciFact (Wadden et al., ACL 2020): Gold standard for scientific NLP — but limited to single-sentence claims verified against single abstracts. No cross-paper contradiction resolution. No argument structure.
  • MultiVerS (Wadden et al., NAACL 2022): Extends to multi-document but uses flat paragraph-level evidence. No SAG, no epistemic scoring, no uncertainty calibration. Binary/ternary labels only.
  • Argument Mining (Stab & Gurevych, 2014; Lawrence & Reed, 2019): Models argument structure in persuasive essays, student essays, debate transcripts — not scientific text. Cannot handle citation-linked evidence chains.
  • SciArg (Lauscher et al., 2018): Argument annotation in scientific abstracts — single-document only, no cross-paper reasoning, no verification task.
  • GPT-4 Fact-Checking: Hallucination-prone for domain-specific claims. Cannot distinguish study design quality. No epistemic calibration. Single-pass — no structured reasoning.
🔬
Research Gap — Explicit and Defensible
What is provably absent from the 2024–2025 literature
  • Gap 1: No system jointly models claim → evidence → rebuttal → qualifier chains across multiple scientific papers. Cross-paper argumentative discourse modeling does not exist in the NLP literature.
  • Gap 2: Scientific claim verification ignores study design quality entirely. MultiVerS treats an expert opinion and a 10,000-person RCT as equally credible. This is scientifically indefensible and technically trivial to address — but no paper has done it.
  • Gap 3: No benchmark for cross-paper contradictory scientific claim pairs with argumentative structure annotations. SciContra does not exist. This gap is provable by literature search.
  • Gap 4: Epistemic uncertainty quantification based on evidence quality is absent from all scientific NLP systems. The output "this claim has 68% confidence based on 3 supporting RCTs vs. 2 contradicting observational studies" has never been produced by an NLP system.
  • Gap 5: Graph Neural Networks have not been applied to scientific argument graph reasoning. GNNs over knowledge graphs are well-studied — but SAGs with typed argumentative relations are a new graph formalism.
  • Gap 6: The intersection of argument mining + scientific NLP has fewer than 5 papers — an explicitly under-exploited research area with high publication probability for well-executed work.

Architecture-Level Design

⚙️
System Architecture & Pipeline
INPUT: Scientific claim to verify
       e.g., "Regular low-dose aspirin reduces colorectal cancer incidence"

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STAGE 1: RETRIEVAL
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Encoder: SciBERT (biomedical domain)
  Index:   FAISS over PubMed abstracts (Contriever embeddings)
  Output:  Top-K papers {P_1, ..., P_K}
           + metadata: {study_type, sample_size, year, journal_IF}
  K = 15 papers (tunable hyperparameter)

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STAGE 2: SCIENTIFIC ARGUMENT GRAPH (SAG) CONSTRUCTION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  For each paper P_i:
    Step A: Named Entity Recognition
            → Identify: [Claims] [Findings] [Methods] [Population]
    Step B: Relation Extraction (SciBERT + fine-tuned classifier)
            → Label relations: supports | contradicts | qualifies | irrelevant
    Step C: Cross-paper coreference resolution
            → Merge nodes referring to same entity across papers

  SAG Node Types:
    [CLAIM]    → The target claim or sub-claims from papers
    [EVIDENCE] → Quantitative findings (β=-0.23, p=0.003, OR=0.78)
    [REBUTTAL] → Counter-evidence or methodological critique
    [QUALIFIER] → Boundary conditions (e.g., "only in patients >60")

  SAG Edge Types:
    supports →   contradicts →   qualifies →   strengthens →

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STAGE 3: EPISTEMIC UNCERTAINTY SCORER
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  For each evidence node E_i, compute study quality weight w_i:

  Study Type Weight (Q_i):
    RCT with pre-registration:  5.0
    RCT:                        4.0
    Cohort study:               3.0
    Case-control:               2.5
    Cross-sectional:            2.0
    Case study / Opinion:       1.0

  Statistical Weight (ST_i):
    Sample_size_score = log(n) / log(max_n)     [normalized]
    P_value_score     = 1 - p_value              [lower p = higher weight]
    Effect_size_score = Cohen's d / max_d        [normalized]
    ST_i = (sample_size_score + p_value_score + effect_size_score) / 3

  Node Weight: w_i = α·Q_i + β·ST_i              [α=0.6, β=0.4 initially]

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STAGE 4: GNN REASONING OVER SAG
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Architecture: Relational Graph Convolutional Network (R-GCN)
  Framework:    PyTorch Geometric
  Layers:       3 R-GCN layers + attention pooling

  Node features:
    - SciBERT embedding (768-dim)
    - Node type embedding (4-dim one-hot)
    - Epistemic weight w_i (scalar)

  Message passing:
    h_i^{l+1} = σ( Σ_{r∈R} Σ_{j∈N_r(i)} (1/c_{i,r}) W_r^l h_j^l + W_0^l h_i^l )

  Global readout: attention-weighted mean over all nodes
  Classification head: 3-class [Supported, Refuted, Contested]

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STAGE 5: CALIBRATION + OUTPUT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Temperature scaling: T* = argmin ECE(val_set)
  Calibrated logits → confidence scores

OUTPUT:
  Verdict:     [Supported | Refuted | Contested]
  Confidence:  0.0 – 1.0  (calibrated, ECE < 0.08 target)
  Evidence Chain: "3 RCTs (total n=48,000) support | 2 cohort studies qualify"
  SAG Visualization: JSON export for inspection

MODELS:    SciBERT, PubMedBERT, R-GCN (PyTorch Geometric)
DATASETS:  SciFact, CORD-19, MultiVerS, SciContra (custom)
COMPUTE:   1× RTX 3090 or A100 (accessible, est. $200–400 cloud)
📦
Datasets
  • SciFact (Wadden et al., 2020): 1,409 claims + 5,183 abstracts. Primary benchmark — use for SAG construction evaluation and main F1 comparison. Fully public, well-understood baselines.
  • MultiVerS datasets: FEVER-Science, CovidFact, HealthVer, Evidence Inference. Used for multi-domain evaluation and R-GCN generalization testing.
  • CORD-19: 400,000+ COVID-19 papers. Used for large-scale SAG construction stress testing and retrieval evaluation.
  • SciContra (this project): 2,000 contradictory claim pairs with Toulmin-structure argument annotations. 50% automated (from systematic review conflicts), 50% expert-annotated. Key differentiator — dataset alone is publishable.
  • SciArg (Lauscher et al., 2018): Argument annotation in abstracts — used for SAG node type classifier pre-training.
🤖
Models & Implementation
  • SciBERT (Beltagy et al., 2019): Base encoder for all NLP components — pretrained on 1.14M scientific papers. Available on HuggingFace, well-supported.
  • PubMedBERT (Gu et al., 2021): Biomedical-specific encoder — used for node feature initialization in medical claims. Better than SciBERT on PubMed-domain tasks.
  • R-GCN (Schlichtkrull et al., 2018): Relational GCN for heterogeneous graphs. PyTorch Geometric implementation. Handles multiple edge types natively — essential for SAG typed edges.
  • Temperature Scaling (Guo et al., 2017): Single-parameter calibration — computationally cheap, well-validated, standard in calibration literature. Applied post-hoc to GNN outputs.
  • Contriever (Izacard et al., 2022): Dense retrieval encoder. FAISS index over PubMed abstracts. Outperforms BM25 on scientific retrieval tasks.

12-Month Timeline
  • Months 1–2: Literature review. SciFact + MultiVerS baseline reproduction. SAG schema design (node types, edge types, Toulmin framework mapping). SciContra annotation guidelines.
  • Months 3–4: SciContra dataset construction (automated conflict mining from systematic reviews + expert annotation of 400 pairs + model-assisted expansion to 2,000). Inter-annotator agreement validation.
  • Months 5–6: SAG construction pipeline (SciBERT NER + relation extraction + cross-paper coreference). Epistemic Uncertainty Scorer implementation and validation against expert ratings.
  • Months 7–8: R-GCN implementation over SAG. Training on SciFact. Core experiments on SciFact + SciContra. Temperature scaling calibration.
  • Months 9–10: Full ablation study. Multi-domain evaluation (CovidFact, HealthVer). Human expert evaluation (50 contested claims, 3 domain experts). Statistical testing.
  • Months 11–12: Paper writing (ACL/EMNLP format). Supervisor review. Submission. FYDP final report and presentation.
⚠️
Risks & Mitigations
Honest — lower risk than any other Q1 idea in this set
  • Risk 1 (MEDIUM): Without SciContra + Epistemic Scorer, the paper looks incremental vs. MultiVerS. Mitigation: Both components are non-negotiable. If one fails, do not submit — pivot to Idea #3. Plan: if SciContra construction falls behind in Month 4, raise alarm immediately.
  • Risk 2 (LOW-MEDIUM): SAG construction quality on heterogeneous scientific text. SciBERT NER on biomedical text is well-studied but argument role labeling is harder. Mitigation: Start with SciArg annotation schema (validated) and fine-tune on manually annotated 300 examples from SciContra.
  • Risk 3 (LOW): R-GCN training instability on small graphs. Mitigation: Use PyTorch Geometric's well-tested R-GCN implementation. Fallback: Graph Attention Network (GAT) — similar architecture, even better-supported. Switch costs 2 days, not 2 weeks.
  • Risk 4 (LOW): Scientific NLP is competitive — MultiVerS is a strong baseline. Mitigation: Our contribution is differentiated on 3 dimensions MultiVerS cannot compete on: SAG structure, epistemic scoring, and SciContra benchmark. Reviewers will see these as complementary, not competing.
  • Compute (NEGLIGIBLE): SciBERT + R-GCN runs on a single RTX 3090. Estimated training time: 4–6 hours per experiment run. Zero cloud credit dependency.
